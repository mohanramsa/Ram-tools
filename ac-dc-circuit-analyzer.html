⚠️
Phasor Diagram — Not Applicable in DC Mode
Phasor diagrams represent rotating vectors and only exist for AC (sinusoidal) circuits.
In DC mode: f = 0 Hz, so XL = 2πfL = 0 (inductor → short circuit) and XC = 1/(2πfC) = ∞ (capacitor → open circuit).
There is no phase angle, no rotating vectors, and no phasor diagram in DC circuits.

📘 Series Circuit — How it works

In a series circuit, all components are connected end-to-end. The same current flows through each component. Total resistance = R1 + R2. Voltage divides across each resistor proportional to its resistance. If any component fails (open), the entire circuit breaks.

Key Formulas Used Internally
Xc = 1 / (2π · f · C) — Capacitive Reactance
XL = 2π · f · L — Inductive Reactance
Z = √(R² + (XL − Xc)²) — Impedance
φ = arctan((XL − Xc) / R) — Phase Angle
f₀ = 1 / (2π · √(LC)) — Resonance Frequency

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the difference between AC mode and DC mode in this analyzer?
In DC mode, only resistance matters — capacitors block DC (very high Xc) and inductors pass DC freely (zero XL). Results show pure resistive current and voltage drops. In AC mode, frequency is active and reactance (Xc and XL) is calculated, giving you impedance, phase angle, power factor, and waveform results. For RC, RL, and RLC circuits, always use AC mode.
How do I read the phasor diagram for an RLC circuit?
The phasor diagram shows four vectors from the origin: VR (red, horizontal — in phase with current), VL (blue, pointing up — leads current by 90°), VC (green, pointing down — lags current by 90°), and V total (purple dashed — the resultant supply voltage). The phase angle φ shown in orange is the angle between V total and VR. Positive φ = inductive circuit; negative φ = capacitive circuit.
What does "⚡ RESONANCE!" mean when it appears in the RLC display?
Resonance in a series RLC circuit occurs when inductive reactance (XL) equals capacitive reactance (Xc). At this resonant frequency (f₀ = 1/2π√LC), they cancel each other out and the circuit impedance equals just R — the minimum possible. This causes maximum current. The analyzer highlights this with a purple glow on the wires and shows the exact f₀ value in the RLC info panel. Try adjusting the frequency slider until XL and Xc match!
Why is the circuit showing zero current even when I adjust the sliders?
The most common reason is that the Circuit Switch is still set to OFF. Find the Circuit Switch toggle in the right-side controls panel and turn it ON. When the switch is OFF, the circuit is open — no current flows, exactly like a physical switch being open. Turn it ON to complete the circuit and see all live readings.
What do Xc, XL, and Z mean in the results?
Xc is capacitive reactance (Ω) — a capacitor's opposition to AC. It decreases as frequency increases (Xc = 1/2πfC). XL is inductive reactance (Ω) — an inductor's opposition to AC. It increases with frequency (XL = 2πfL). Z is total impedance (Ω) — the combined opposition of resistance and net reactance: Z = √(R² + (XL−Xc)²). In DC circuits, Z = R since Xc and XL are zero.
Can I analyze a simple DC resistor circuit with this tool?
Yes. Select the 🔴 Series Circuit tab and switch to 🔋 DC mode. Set the Voltage (V), R1, and R2 sliders to your desired values. Turn the Circuit Switch ON. The tool displays total resistance (R1 + R2), current (I = V/R by Ohm's Law), and the individual voltage drops across each resistor — all in real time.
Is this tool accurate enough for real engineering calculations?
Yes, for educational, conceptual, and preliminary engineering purposes. The formulas used match standard AC circuit analysis theory exactly as taught in engineering textbooks. For safety-critical final design work — high-voltage systems, production hardware — always validate with professional simulation software (like MATLAB, LTspice) and physical measurements. RamTools is perfect for learning, teaching, and rapid design estimation.
